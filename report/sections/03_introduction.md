# Introduction

## Context: India’s Stagnant Air Quality Crisis
India remains home to some of the world's most polluted cities. Despite the launch of the National Clean Air Programme (NCAP) and the implementation of varied measures such as the Graded Response Action Plan (GRAP), BS-VI vehicle transitions, and odd-even schemes, the national average for PM2.5 has remained virtually unchanged over the last six years, hovering around **59 µg/m³** (2018–2023).

## The Problem: City-Centric Policy vs. Regional Health Burden
Current air quality management in India is heavily city-centric, focusing primarily on "non-attainment" cities like Delhi. However, health impacts are non-linear and are significantly compounded by factors beyond raw concentration levels, such as seasonality, population density, and baseline socioeconomic vulnerability. There is an urgent need to understand how pollution propagates across district boundaries and how it causally drives health outcomes at a more granular level.

## Research Gap
Existing literature in India often relies on short-term city-level studies or broad national-level correlations. There is a lack of rigorous, long-term (multi-year), district-level studies that:
1.  **Move from correlation to causation** using quasi-experimental methods.
2.  **Incorporate spatial graph theory** to understand geographic interdependencies.
3.  **Identify socioeconomic compounders** that make certain regions more vulnerable to "moderate" pollution levels than others.

## Objectives
This research aims to:
-   **Robustly quantify** the impact of PM2.5, PM10, NO₂, and SO₂ on respiratory and cardiovascular health outcomes across 150 districts.
-   **Map spatial vulnerabilities** using graph-based centrality and autocorrelation measures.
-   **Establish causal precedence** between pollution spikes and health burden.
-   **Provide evidence-based policy recommendations** for shifting from city-centric monitoring to vulnerability-based spatial clustering.
