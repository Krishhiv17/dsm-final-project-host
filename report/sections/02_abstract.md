# Abstract

This research presents a comprehensive, multi-methodological analysis of the relationship between ambient air quality and public health outcomes across 150 districts in 15 Indian states from 2018 to 2023. Leveraging a massive dataset comprising ~328,000 air quality records (CPCB) and 10,800 monthly health indicators (HMIS), we employ a three-pronged analytical framework integrating **Machine Learning**, **Spatial Graph Theory**, and **Causal Inference**. 

Our predictive models achieve high accuracy (Random Forest R² = 0.81) in forecasting respiratory disease burden, identifying population and particulate matter (PM2.5, PM10) as the primary drivers. Beyond correlation (Pearson r = 0.38, p < 0.001), we establish temporal precedence using **Granger Causality** and **Cross-Correlation**, finding that respiratory cases typically peak approximately one month following pollution spikes. 

Furthermore, **Spatial Autocorrelation (Moran’s I ≈ 0.5)** reveals significant geographic clustering of disease. A key finding is the identification of a **"Socioeconomic Compounding" anomaly (Cluster 3)**: districts in Uttar Pradesh and Bihar exhibit catastrophic disease rates (2-3× higher than Delhi) despite having only moderate PM2.5 levels (~70 µg/m³), suggesting that poverty, population density, and baseline health significantly amplify pollution’s harm. Despite numerous policy interventions, national PM2.5 levels remained stagnant (~59 µg/m³) over the six-year period. These results underscore the urgent need for NCAP to shift from city-centric targets to high-vulnerability spatial clusters.
